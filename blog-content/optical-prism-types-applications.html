Introduction to Optical Prisms

Optical prisms are transparent solid glass elements with flat polished surfaces that refract, reflect, or disperse light. They are fundamental components in optical systems, used for beam steering, image rotation, dispersion, and many other applications.

Right Angle Prisms

The most common prism type, featuring a 90-degree angle between two legs. Right angle prisms can be used to bend light by 90 degrees (using one reflection) or 180 degrees (using two reflections). They are also used as mirrors, retroreflectors, and in periscope systems.

Key applications: Beam steering, image rotation, periscope systems, optical instruments.

Penta Prisms

Penta prisms deviate light by exactly 90 degrees regardless of the angle of incidence (within limits). Unlike right angle prisms, the 90-degree deviation does not change with prism rotation, making them ideal for alignment-critical applications.

Key applications: Optical tooling, surveying instruments, alignment systems, viewfinders.

Dove Prisms

Dove prisms are truncated right angle prisms that rotate an image by twice the angle of prism rotation. They are used for image rotation and have an interesting property: rotating the prism by angle θ rotates the image by 2θ.

Key applications: Image rotation, pattern recognition, astronomical instruments.

Corner Cube Prisms (Retroreflectors)

Corner cube prisms reflect any incident beam back to its source, regardless of the angle of incidence (within the clear aperture). This retroreflection property makes them invaluable for distance measurement and alignment.

Key applications: Laser ranging, interferometry, satellite tracking, road reflectors.

Equilateral Prisms

Equilateral (60-degree) prisms are primarily used for dispersion - separating white light into its constituent wavelengths. The angular spread depends on the glass type and wavelength.

Key applications: Spectroscopy, wavelength selection, educational demonstrations.

Wedge Prisms

Wedge prisms have a small angle between their surfaces, used for beam steering by refraction. A pair of wedge prisms can provide continuous beam steering over a range of angles.

Key applications: Beam steering, variable beam offset, laser tuning.
