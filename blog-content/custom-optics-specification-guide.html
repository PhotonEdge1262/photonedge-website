How to Specify Custom Optics: A Complete Engineering Guide

May 15, 2025 12 min read PhotonEdge Engineering Team

Introduction

Custom optical components are essential for demanding applications where standard, off-the-shelf optics cannot meet performance requirements. However, the process of specifying custom optics can be challenging for engineers who are not optical specialists. A poorly specified custom optic can lead to increased cost, extended lead times, or components that fail to meet application requirements.

This comprehensive guide walks engineers through the key parameters and considerations when specifying custom optical components. From material selection to surface quality specifications, we cover everything you need to know to ensure your custom optics are manufactured to meet your exact requirements.

1. Understanding Your Application Requirements

Before specifying any optical component, you must have a clear understanding of your application's requirements. Key questions to answer include:

  • Wavelength range: What wavelength(s) will the optics be used with? This determines both material selection and coating requirements.
  • Optical power: What power levels will the optics handle? This affects material choice and coating damage thresholds.
  • Environmental conditions: What temperatures, humidity levels, and atmospheric conditions will the optics experience?
  • Precision requirements: What level of surface quality, flatness, or wavefront distortion can your system tolerate?
  • Budget and timeline: What cost constraints and delivery requirements apply to your project?

2. Material Selection

Choosing the right optical material is fundamental to component performance. Consider the following properties:

2.1 Transmission Range

Different materials transmit different wavelength ranges. Match your material to your wavelength:

  • N-BK7 (K9): 350nm - 2μm (best for visible and near-IR)
  • UV Fused Silica (JGS1, JGS2): 185nm - 2.5μm (excellent UV transmission)
  • CaF₂: 130nm - 8μm (broadband UV to mid-IR)
  • ZnSe: 600nm - 20μm (ideal for CO₂ lasers)
  • Ge: 2μm - 16μm (LWIR applications)
  • Diamond: 250nm - far-IR and beyond (ultra-high power handling)

2.2 Thermal Properties

For applications with temperature variations, consider thermal expansion coefficient and thermal conductivity:

  • Fused Silica: Very low thermal expansion (0.52 × 10⁻⁶/K), excellent thermal shock resistance
  • N-BK7: Moderate thermal expansion (7.2 × 10⁻⁶/K), suitable for stable environments
  • CaF₂: Low expansion, but sensitive to thermal shock

2.3 Refractive Index and Homogeneity

For precision imaging or interferometric applications, refractive index homogeneity becomes critical. Specify index homogeneity based on your application:

  • Standard grade: ±5 × 10⁻⁴ (general purpose)
  • Precision grade: ±1 × 10⁻⁴ (imaging systems)
  • High precision: ±5 × 10⁻⁵ (interferometry, lithography)

3. Surface Quality Specifications

Surface quality is specified using the scratch-dig standard (MIL-PRF-13830B), expressed as two numbers (e.g., 20-10):

  • First number (Scratch): Maximum width of scratches in micrometers
  • Second number (Dig): Maximum diameter of pits/digs in micrometers

Common specifications and applications:

SpecificationApplication
80-50Commercial imaging, illumination
60-40General purpose optics, laboratory
40-20Laser systems, scientific imaging
20-10Laser cavities, interferometers
10-5 or betterMetrology, lithography, research

4. Surface Flatness and Wavefront Distortion

Surface flatness is measured in waves (λ) at a reference wavelength, typically 632.8nm (HeNe laser):

  • λ/2: Standard commercial quality
  • λ/4: Good imaging and general laser optics
  • λ/10: Precision laser cavities, interferometric applications
  • λ/20: Metrology-grade optics, laser resonators
  • λ/40 to λ/100: Ultra-precision for research, lithography

5. Dimensional and Angular Tolerances

5.1 Diameter and Thickness Tolerances

Standard tolerances:

  • Commercial: ±0.25mm diameter, ±0.25mm thickness
  • Precision: ±0.10mm diameter, ±0.10mm thickness
  • High precision: ±0.05mm diameter, ±0.05mm thickness

5.2 Angle Tolerances

For prisms and wedge plates:

  • Commercial: ±1 arcmin
  • Precision: ±30 arcsec
  • High precision: ±5 arcsec or better

5.3 Centration

Lens centration is measured as beam deviation or decenter:

  • Standard: <3 arcmin deviation
  • Precision: <1 arcmin deviation
  • High precision: <30 arcsec deviation

6. Coating Specifications

6.1 Anti-Reflection (AR) Coatings

Specify AR coatings by wavelength range:

  • Single-layer MgF₂: 350-700nm, ~1.5% reflectance per surface
  • VIS-NIR BBAR: 400-700nm or 600-1000nm, <0.5% reflectance
  • MWIR/LWIR BBAR: 3-5μm or 8-12μm, <2% reflectance
  • Custom wavelength: Specify exact wavelength range and maximum reflectance

6.2 High-Reflectance (HR) Coatings

For laser mirrors and reflectors:

  • Laser line HR: >99.5% at specific wavelength (1064nm, 532nm, etc.)
  • Broadband HR: >99% over wavelength range
  • Specify: Wavelength, minimum reflectance, bandwidth, laser damage threshold requirement

6.3 Coating Durability

Consider environmental requirements:

  • Standard durability: MIL-C-675 adhesion test
  • Military durability: MIL-STD-810 environmental testing
  • High humidity: 85% RH @ 85°C testing

7. Laser Damage Threshold (LIDT) Requirements

For high-power laser applications, specify LIDT requirements:

  • Measurement standard: ISO 21254 or MIL-PRF-13830B
  • Test wavelength: Match your laser wavelength
  • Pulse duration: CW, nanosecond, picosecond, or femtosecond
  • Minimum threshold: Specify minimum acceptable LIDT value (J/cm² or W/cm²)

8. Testing and Inspection Requirements

Specify required testing documentation:

  • Certificate of Conformance (CoC): Standard compliance certification
  • Test reports: Interferogram data, spectrophotometry curves
  • First article inspection: Full dimensional verification
  • Sample approval: Pre-production samples for critical applications

9. Documentation Best Practices

When requesting custom optics quotes, provide:

  1. Technical drawing with all dimensions, tolerances, and specifications
  2. Material specification or acceptable alternatives
  3. Surface quality requirement with applicable standard
  4. Coating specification including spectral performance requirements
  5. Quantity and projected annual volume
  6. Application description to help manufacturer suggest optimizations
  7. Sample or reference part if available

10. Common Specification Mistakes to Avoid

  • Over-specifying: Unnecessarily tight tolerances increase cost and lead time without benefit
  • Under-specifying: Missing requirements lead to parts that don't meet needs
  • Ignoring beam alignment: Specify centration for all lenses
  • Forgetting coatings: Uncoated surfaces lose significant light at air-glass interfaces
  • No LIDT specification: Critical for laser applications
  • Vague descriptions: "Best quality" or "high precision" mean different things to different manufacturers

Conclusion

Properly specifying custom optics requires careful consideration of multiple parameters. By understanding your application requirements and communicating them clearly, you can ensure your custom optical components meet your exact needs while optimizing cost and delivery time.
