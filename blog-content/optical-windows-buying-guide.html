Introduction

Optical windows are flat, parallel-sided components that allow light to pass through while protecting internal optics from environmental factors. Whether you are building a laser system, imaging device, or sensor, choosing the right optical window is crucial. Here are the five key specifications you must check.

1. Surface Flatness

Surface flatness measures how closely the window surface approaches a perfect plane, typically specified in fractions of a wavelength (e.g., λ/4, λ/10, λ/20 at 632.8nm). Flatter surfaces produce less wavefront distortion.

  • λ/2 - λ/4: General-purpose applications
  • λ/4 - λ/10: Imaging and measurement systems
  • λ/10 - λ/20: Interferometry and high-precision applications

Tip: Overspecifying flatness significantly increases cost. Match the specification to your actual wavefront requirements.

2. Parallelism

Parallelism is the angular tolerance between the two surfaces of the window. Poor parallelism causes beam deviation and ghost images. Typical specifications range from 1 arc minute (standard) to <10 arc seconds (precision).

For laser applications, tighter parallelism reduces beam walk-off. For imaging, it minimizes double-image artifacts.

3. Material Transmission

The window material must transmit efficiently at your operating wavelength. Common options include:

  • BK7: 350-2000nm (visible/NIR)
  • UV Fused Silica: 185-2500nm (UV to NIR)
  • Sapphire: 200-5000nm (rugged, wide range)
  • Germanium: 2000-14000nm (thermal IR)
  • ZnSe: 600-16000nm (CO2 laser)
  • CaF2: 180-8000nm (deep UV to IR)

4. Clear Aperture

Clear aperture defines the usable area of the window that meets specifications. Typical clear aperture is >85-90% of the outer diameter. Ensure the clear aperture covers your beam or field of view with adequate margin.

5. Surface Quality

Surface quality is specified using scratch-dig notation (e.g., 40-20, 20-10, 10-5). The first number is the maximum scratch width, and the second is the maximum dig diameter. For most applications, 40-20 is sufficient. Laser and imaging applications often require 20-10 or better.
