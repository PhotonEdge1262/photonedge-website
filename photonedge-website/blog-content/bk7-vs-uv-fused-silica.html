Overview

BK7 and UV fused silica are the two most commonly used optical glass materials, but they serve very different purposes. Understanding their differences is essential for choosing the right material for your optical system.

BK7 (SCHOTT) / K9 (Chinese Equivalent)

BK7 is a borosilicate crown glass with excellent optical properties for visible and near-infrared applications:

  • Transmission range: 350nm - 2000nm
  • Refractive index (nd): 1.5168
  • Abbe number: 64.17
  • Thermal expansion: 7.1 × 10³¹⁄⁶ /°C
  • Key advantages: Cost-effective, excellent homogeneity, widely available, easy to fabricate
  • Best for: Visible imaging, general-purpose optics, low-power laser systems

UV Fused Silica (JGS1)

UV fused silica is a synthetic amorphous silicon dioxide with outstanding UV transmission and thermal stability:

  • Transmission range: 185nm - 2500nm
  • Refractive index (nd): 1.4585
  • Abbe number: 67.82
  • Thermal expansion: 0.55 × 10³¹⁄⁶ /°C
  • Key advantages: Deep UV transmission, extremely low thermal expansion, high laser damage threshold, excellent fluorescence characteristics
  • Best for: UV applications, high-power laser systems, precision metrology, fluorescence microscopy

When to Choose BK7

  • Your application is in the visible or near-IR range only (above 350nm)
  • Cost is a primary concern and UV performance is not needed
  • You need a high refractive index for optical design flexibility
  • Standard imaging applications without extreme thermal requirements

When to Choose UV Fused Silica

  • Your application requires UV transmission below 350nm
  • High-power laser systems where thermal lensing must be minimized
  • Precision interferometry where thermal stability is critical
  • Applications requiring minimal fluorescence
  • Environments with large temperature fluctuations

Cost Comparison

BK7 is typically 2-5 times less expensive than UV fused silica for the same geometry. However, the total cost of an optical system should consider that UV fused silica's superior properties may allow fewer elements or simpler designs, potentially offsetting the material cost difference.

Conclusion

Both materials have their place in optical design. Choose BK7 for cost-effective visible-range applications, and UV fused silica whenever UV transmission, thermal stability, or laser power handling is required. Contact our team for material selection guidance.
