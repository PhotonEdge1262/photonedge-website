Introduction

Precision optical components are only as good as their verified quality. A lens with a claimed λ/10 surface flatness, a mirror with 99.9% reflectance, or a window with 20-10 surface quality must be tested to confirm those specifications before they are integrated into a critical optical system.

Optical quality testing encompasses a range of metrology techniques that verify dimensional accuracy, surface form, coating performance, and material properties. Understanding these testing methods helps engineers and procurement teams evaluate supplier claims, qualify new components, and establish quality acceptance criteria.

This guide covers the most important optical quality testing methods, the international standards that govern them, and how PhotonEdge implements quality assurance across our product line.

Surface Form Testing: Interferometry

Interferometer testing is the gold standard for measuring the surface figure of precision optics—lenses, mirrors, windows, and prisms. Interferometry compares the wavefront reflected from (or transmitted through) the test surface against a reference surface, producing interference fringes that reveal surface deviations with sub-wavelength accuracy.

Types of Interferometers

  • Fizeau interferometer — Uses a reference flat or sphere placed close to (but not touching) the test surface. The gap between the two surfaces creates interference fringes. The most common method for measuring surface flatness of mirrors and windows. Accuracy: λ/20 to λ/100 (at 632.8nm HeNe wavelength).
  • Twyman-Green interferometer — Tests the complete wavefront transmitted through or reflected from an optical component. Ideal for measuring lenses, prisms, and assembled optics. The test beam is compared against a reference beam to reveal aberrations introduced by the component.
  • Laser Fizeau interferometer — Uses a coherent laser source for high-precision measurements. The 632.8nm HeNe wavelength is standard; UV (257nm) or IR (1053nm) sources are used for special applications. Results are often expressed in waves (e.g., λ/10 = 0.1 waves P-V).
  • Phase-shifting interferometer (PSI) — Captures multiple interferograms while shifting the phase of the reference wavefront. Computer algorithms reconstruct a full surface map with nanometer-level height resolution. Superior to traditional fringe counting for smooth, high-precision surfaces.

Reading Interferograms

Interferograms display surface form as a pattern of bright and dark fringes. Each fringe represents a height change of λ/2 (half the test wavelength):

  • Straight, equally spaced fringes — Perfectly flat surface (or sphere centered on-axis)
  • Curved fringes — Surface has cylindrical or spherical power (curvature)
  • Irregular spacing — Local surface irregularities (scratches, digs, mid-spatial frequency errors)
  • Fringe count (N) — Total number of fringes across the aperture gives peak-to-valley (P-V) deviation: P-V = N × (λ/2)

Common surface flatness specifications for precision optics:

  • λ/2 (≈0.3 waves at 632.8nm) — Standard commercial quality
  • λ/4 (≈0.25 waves) — Good imaging and general laser optics
  • λ/10 (≈0.1 waves) — Precision laser cavities and interferometric applications
  • λ/20 (≈0.05 waves) — Metrology-grade optics and laser resonators
  • λ/40 to λ/100 — Ultra-precision optics for research, lithography, and gravitational wave detection

Surface Quality Inspection: Scratch-Dig Standards

Surface quality inspection evaluates the cosmetic condition of an optical surface—the presence of scratches, pits (digs), bubbles, and inclusions. Surface defects scatter light, reduce contrast in imaging systems, and can cause localized coating stress leading to damage in laser applications.

Surface quality is specified using the scratch-dig standard, expressed as two numbers (e.g., 20-10, 60-40, 10-5):

  • First number (Scratch) — Width of the largest scratch in micrometers (μm). A 20-scratch means the widest scratch is no wider than 20μm.
  • Second number (Dig) — Diameter of the largest pit/dig in micrometers. A 10-dig means the largest dig is no larger than 10μm diameter.

Scratch-Dig Inspection Methods

  • Visual comparison method — The inspector views the surface under a high-intensity lamp at a defined angle, comparing against standardized scratch-dig reference samples. This is the most common method but is subjective and operator-dependent.
  • Optical scattering method — Illuminates the surface at an angle and measures scattered light intensity using a photo detector. More objective and repeatable than visual inspection. Used for high-precision and laser-grade optics.
  • Microscopy — Optical or confocal microscopy provides direct measurement of scratch width and dig diameter. Used for detailed defect analysis and dispute resolution. Capable of measuring defects down to 1μm.

Common Scratch-Dig Grades and Applications

  • 80-50 — Commercial quality: imaging lenses, illumination optics
  • 60-40 — Good quality: general-purpose optics, laboratory use
  • 40-20 — Precision quality: laser systems, scientific imaging
  • 20-10 — High precision: laser cavities, interferometers, microscopy
  • 10-5 or better — Ultra-precision: lithography, metrology, research

Thickness, Parallelism, and Dimensional Testing

Beyond surface form and cosmetic quality, optical components must meet mechanical and geometric tolerances:

  • Central thickness — Measured with a contact micrometer or non-contact displacement sensor. Critical for lenses where thickness affects focal length and weight.
  • Center thickness tolerance — Typically ±0.05mm to ±0.25mm for commercial lenses, ±0.01mm for precision components.
  • Parallelism — Measured by rotating the window between two parallel surfaces or using an autocollimator. Critical for laser windows and etalons where angular deviation causes beam walk-off. Specified in arcminutes (′) or arcseconds (″): typically <3′ for precision windows, <30″ for laser-quality windows.
  • Surface angle — Prism angles measured using a precision angle gauge or goniometer. Critical for dispersing prisms, dove prisms, and beam displacers. Precision prisms require <±1 arcminute tolerance.
  • Diameter and centering — Verified with precision calipers or coordinate measuring machines (CMM). Lens centering (decenter of the optical axis relative to the mechanical axis) is measured on a centering telescope.

Coating Performance Testing

Optical coatings must be tested to confirm they meet design specifications:

  • Spectral performance — Measured using a spectrophotometer (UV-Vis-NIR or FTIR) to verify reflectance and transmittance at the design wavelength(s). Results compared against the coating design specification.
  • Laser-induced damage threshold (LIDT) testing — Critical for laser grade optics. Components are exposed to increasing laser fluence until damage is observed. Reported in J/cm² (pulsed) or W/cm² (CW). Standardized test protocols include ISO 21254 and MIL-PRF-13830B.
  • Adhesion testing — Tape pull tests verify coating adhesion to the substrate per MIL-C-675. Critical for durability assessment in demanding environments.
  • Environmental testing — Coated optics are subjected to humidity (85% RH, 85°C), thermal cycling (-40°C to +70°C), and salt spray tests per MIL-STD-810 to verify coating durability.

International Standards for Optical Quality

Several international standards govern optical quality testing and specification:

  • MIL-PRF-13830B — U.S. military performance specification for optical components. Defines scratch-dig standards, surface flatness measurement, and general quality requirements. Widely referenced in commercial optics procurement.
  • ISO 10110 — International standard for optical drawing notation. Part 1 (General), Part 7 (Surface imperfection), Part 5 (Surface form), Part 12 (Optical elements made of colorless optical glass). Provides a standardized drawing language for specifying optical components.
  • ISO 21254 — Laser and laser-related equipment: test methods for laser-induced damage threshold. Defines LIDT measurement procedures and reporting requirements.
  • DIN 3140 — German standard for optical quality grades, similar in concept to scratch-dig. Defines quality grades Q1–Q6 with specific allowable defect sizes.

Material Quality Testing

The optical substrate material itself must meet quality standards:

  • Strain point and annealing — Internal stress in glass substrates causes birefringence. Measured using a polarimeter or strain viewer. Low-strain annealed glass is essential for polarization-sensitive and interferometric applications.
  • Inclusion and bubble testing — Glass samples are inspected under dark-field illumination for bubbles, inclusions, and seeds per ISO 10110-7.
  • Transmittance and homogeneity — Spectrophotometer measurements verify that the material transmits the intended wavelength range without absorption bands. Refractive index homogeneity (measured by interferometry through the material) is critical for high-power laser glass and lithographic optics.

Conclusion

Verifying optical component quality requires a combination of surface form testing (interferometry), cosmetic inspection (scratch-dig), dimensional verification, coating performance testing, and material characterization. Understanding these testing methods enables better specification writing, more effective supplier qualification, and confidence that components will perform in your optical system.

When sourcing precision optics, always ask your supplier for test data and verify that testing was performed at your actual operating conditions—not just under ideal laboratory conditions. Requesting certificates of conformance, LIDT reports, and spectral test data is standard practice in the precision optics industry.
